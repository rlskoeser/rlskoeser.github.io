<p><a href="http://web.library.emory.edu/">Emory Libraries</a> <a href="http://marbl.library.emory.edu/">Manuscript, Archives, &amp; Rare Book Library</a> have strong experience in preparing rare works and authors’ notes for sharing with researchers. With the addition of significant sentitive contemporary born-digital archives to their collection, they have gained a new need: digital analogs to the processes and tools they’re using for their paper collections. MARBL have asked Emory Libraries’ Digital Systems group to research digital redaction strategies capabilities and offer this technical recommendation.</p>

<p>This recommendation examines four commercial redaction packages: <a href="http://www.adobe.com/products/acrobatpro/">Adobe Acrobat 9 Professional</a>, <a href="http://www.appligent.com/redax">Appligent Redax 4.6</a>, <a href="http://www.redact-it.com/">Informative Graphics Redact-It</a>, and <a href="http://www.rapidredact.com/">RapidRedact</a>. It also examines and quickly rejects two alternative solutions for redaction: <a href="http://www.imagesolutions.com/isitoolbox/">ISIToolBox Professional</a>, and the MS Office <a href="http://www.microsoft.com/downloads/details.aspx?FamilyId=028C0FD7-67C2-4B51-8E87-65CC9F30F2ED">Word Redaction Add-in</a>. Ultimately it concludes that the best redaction solution for MARBL’s current needs is provided by <a href="http://www.rapidredact.com/">RapidRedact</a>. If that product proves financially infeasible, <a href="http://www.appligent.com/redax">Appligent Redax</a> provides a very strong second choice.</p>

<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>There are a number of issues that are more complicated in digital redaction than they are in physical redaction. Digital documents are easily modified, but they also make it easier to recover original versions. The wealth of opportunities for metadata in digital documents means that these documents often contain information that’s not immediately presented to a user. Converting documents between data formats is an everyday task in the digital world, but these conversions frequently lose subtle data in ways that might not be easily reversible and often aren’t obvious to users.</p>

<p>It’s not all bad news, of course. We create digital documents for the opportunities and possibilities they create for us, and some of these can be very beneficial for redaction and archiving. Most obviously, it’s typically trivial to make a perfect copy of a digital document. This is apparent to adept computer users, but it’s worth mentioning here if only to highlight the counterpoint that not all copies are exact, and that sometimes it takes a technologist to tell the difference between exact and inexact copies.  Another important benefit is searching: It’s usually possible — and often easy — to have a computer program search documents automatically for sensitive words or phrases and handle them appropriately.  Some applications also provide a degree of auditing and can track which users modified which parts of which documents, and when. All of these can provide advantages in the archival process, hopefully offsetting the additional difficulties imposed by the medium.</p> 

<h3>Disadvantage: Recovery of Redacted Content</h3>
<p>With a naïve redaction strategy, it may be possible to completely recover redacted content. Many everyday users benefit from the ability to undo their recent changes, so many document editing applications maintain some editing history inside the file. If a naïve redaction doesn’t account for this, it may be possible for later users to simply undo the redaction and access the original information. All of the redaction strategies described here account for this possibility and completely and irreversibly remove redacted content.</p> 

<p>Additionally, the data structures used to store data sometimes leave remnants of old data still present in difficult-to-access sections of the file. This is a digital analog of scraping old ink from a parchment and reusing it, and it is common practice in many document formats. Just as researchers can carefully examine parchments and sometimes find remnants of old scraped texts, technical users can examine a digital document and sometimes find remnants of old saved data. All of the redaction strategies described here account for this technique: Past records are completely wiped and inaccessible after redaction.</p> 

<h3>Disadvantage: Sensitive Metadata</h3>
<p>Computers store a lot of metadata for their documents. Most of it is not immediately accessible in a document editor; it requires navigating through a series of dialog boxes and configuration windows to find it all. This metadata can be a jackpot of information for curious researchers.  Unfortunately, it’s also notoriously difficult to redact thoroughly. All of the redaction packages described here address this problem by eliminating this metadata entirely from redacted documents.</p> 

<h3>Disadvantage: Format Conversions and Data Loss</h3>
<p>Most professional redaction software operates primarily on Adobe PDF documents and TIFF image documents. Some packages will make this conversion for us; others expect us to make the simple conversion ourselves before redacting. The disadvantage is that the conversion to PDF forces the computer to store formatting and metadata in a different way, making it very difficult to return the redacted document to its original application environment. The original unredacted document will of course be left unchanged and retain its original capabilities; the difficulty lies only in taking the redacted result and importing it back into its original environment.</p>

<p>Image conversion takes this difficulty a step further and converts the document from a searchable text format into a raw image, which lacks not only formatting data but also the capacity for searching. This searchability can be re-added later through further conversions, but these conversions typically remove the document even more from its original condition, making restoration more and more difficult.</p> 

<p>All of the redaction strategies reviewed here require some conversion, save one. The MS Office Word Redaction Add-in redacts Word documents without conversion, though this strategy is rejected for other reasons as described below. Of the remaining packages, some automatically maintain searchable text, while others convert documents to image formats and back. All of them ultimately allow text search, though some may require additional workflow to support it. Details are discussed in the product descriptions below.</p> 

<h3>Advantage: Perfect Copying</h3>
<p>With digital data it is almost always easy for a technical user to make an exact copy of data, with no loss whatsoever. All of the data for this project has been backed up and stored securely, but technological limitations like the conversion problem described above prevent us from providing exact duplicates for redaction. When staff use software to produce redacted result documents, exact copies of these result documents will ultimately be among the documents available to researchers.</p> 

<h3>Advantage: Searching</h3>
<p>One of the advantages that digital documents provide us is the ability to search through large piles of them for the information we want. This benefits researchers, of course, but it also benefits the redaction process.  Many of the tools described here can search through a document or even a whole batch of documents, highlighting or automatically redacting words and phrases deemed sensitive. The resulting document will of course still need to be reviewed by a staff archivist for verification, but we expect this searching to significantly reduce the manpower required for the redaction effort.</p>

<h3>Advantage: Auditing</h3>
<p>Another advantage of digital media is that some redaction tools are able to track the editing of documents, recording who makes changes and when and why they make them. It’s very difficult to make this auditing process absolutely tamperproof in software alone, but the software can provide a strong technological base that administrators can augment with human policies to strengthen auditing and tracking guarantees.</p>

<h2>Products</h2>
<p>This recommendation reviews four digital redaction packages: Adobe Acrobat 9 Professional, Appligent Redax 4.6, Informative Graphics Redact-It, and RapidRedact. It also briefly highlights both ISIToolbox Professional and the MS Office Word Redaction Add-in, though these latter tools are ultimately inappropriate for this project.</p> 

<h3>Adobe Acrobat 9 Professional</h3>
<p><a href="http://www.adobe.com/products/acrobat/">Adobe Acrobat</a> is the premier professional PDF publication tool from the people who literally invented the PDF format. In 2006, with the release of Acrobat 8, Adobe added <a href="http://blogs.adobe.com/acrolaw/2006/10/acrobat_8_new_f_1.html">redaction support</a> to the product.</p>

<p>Acrobat 9 comes in three packages, with increasing numbers of features in increasingly expensive titles. <a href="http://www.adobe.com/products/acrobatstd/">Acrobat Standard</a> is the lowest-end Adobe product with full PDF editing capabilities. <a href="http://www.adobe.com/products/acrobatpro/">Acrobat Pro</a> adds many features and is the least expensive program in the product line to support native redaction capabilities. <a href="http://www.adobe.com/products/acrobatproextended/">Acrobat Pro Extended</a> offers even more features targeted at the high-end web publishing and graphic arts industries.</p> 

<h4>Analysis</h4>
<p>Acrobat 9 Professional is an excellent and well-reputed tool for PDF generation and publishing. It offers features for creating and validating PDF documents for <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PDF/A">archival</a> and accessibility standards conformance. It allows PDF publishers to digitally sign PDFs for cryptographic authenticity, compare PDF documents for differences, and prepare them for high-end print production.</p>

<p>While Acrobat 9 Professional does offer functionality for redaction, it’s not the product’s focus, so its features for redaction projects are somewhat limited. Available documentation doesn’t talk about any sort of audit log for change tracking. It doesn’t appear to support the complex preprogrammed pattern-based redaction features in its more focused competitors. It’s also costly, especially when the cost of support is added to that of the product itself.</p>

<p><a href="http://www.adobe.com/go/buyacrobat_pro">Adobe list Acrobat 9 Professional</a> at $449 per single-user license. Support is available at a cost of $175 for 5 incidents/calls, or $1200 per year for unlimited incidents. <a href="http://www.adobe.com/products/acrobatpro/tryout.html">Short-term trial downloads</a> are available for free.</p>

<p>Adobe Acrobat 9 Professional is the industry standard product for general editing and production of PDF documents. If we already have a copy then we can use it for redaction. Given its cost and limited redaction feature set, though, it is not an ideal choice for current MARBL redaction needs.</p>

<h3>Appligent Redax 4.6</h3>
<p><a href="http://www.appligent.com/redax">Appligent Redax</a> is the oldest and most mature application specifically focused on PDF redaction. It was the tool that Adobe typically recommended to people for redaction prior to their addition of the feature to Acrobat 8.</p>

<p>Redax is an Acrobat plugin, which means it requires Acrobat (Standard or greater) and runs inside that program. In addition to the standard version, Appligent produce <a href="http://www.appligent.com/res">Redax Enterprise Server</a> for groups with large collections of PDF documents for batch reduction.</p>

<h4>Analysis</h4>
<p>Redax is an excellent package focused on PDF redaction. It provides audit logs, complex pattern-based redaction, and a mature user interface for redaction tasks. Like Acrobat, it works with the PDF document as text rather than requiring image conversion. Appligent also offer a programmable batchable Redax Enterprise Server for very large redaction projects.</p>

<p>There are few technical downsides to Redax. It requires Acrobat, where standalone applications can be installed without it, though normally this should be a trivial complication. Several of Acrobat’s features — such as validation of archival and accessibility standarda conformance — are not available in Acrobat 9 Standard, though Redax can run with the more expensive Acrobat 9 Professional if these features are deemed valuable.</p> <p><a href="http://store.appligent.com/WebStore/catalog/product_info.php?products_id=30">Appligent list Redax 4.6</a> for $249, with support contracts available for $49.80 per year, and a discount for buying a three-year support contract up front. It requires Acrobat Standard, which <a href="http://www.adobe.com/go/buyacrobat_standard">Adobe advertise</a> at $299, with the same support availability as described for Acrobat 9 Professional above. Short-term trial downloads are available for free. For pricing of Redax Enterprise Server, contact <a href="http://www.appligent.com/howtobuy">Appligent’s sales department</a>.</p>

<p>Appligent Redax 4.6 has excellent features, a focus on redaction workflow, a mature product, and opportunities for integration into a more complete high-end redaction suite if the project scope grows to demand it.  It is not our primary software recommendation, but it is a very strong secondary offering if our primary recommendation is not feasible.</p>

<h3>Informative Graphics Redact-It</h3>
<p><a href="http://www.redact-it.com/">Redact-It</a> offers a redaction-focused tool with an impressive feature list. Though a relative newcomer to the field, its low price makes it a formidable contender for some redaction projects.</p>

<p>Redact-It is a standalone program: It does not require Adobe Acrobat to function. In addition to Redact-It Desktop, Informative Graphics offers <a href="http://www.redact-it.com/redactit_server.htm">Redact-It Enterprise Server</a> for programmable and batch processing.</p>

<h4>Analysis</h4>
<p>Redact-It advertises an impressive <a href="http://www.redact-it.com/featurebenefits.htm">feature list</a>. It opens a wide variety of source file types, automatically converting them for redaction. It comes pre-programmed with scripts to remove a wide variety of sensitive information like social security numbers and phone numbers, and it allows users to make their own patterns for automated redaction. With their proprietary <a href="http://www.redact-it.com/csf.htm">CSF</a> output format they provide even more options for review and collaboration.</p> 

<p>Unfortunately, their proprietary encrypted CSF format is not helpful for current library projects, which need full access to redaction data. They support output to PDF, but it’s not clear from their documentation if those PDFs include searchable text, or if they’ll require additional workflow to be searchable. I contacted their technical support department for an answer to this question, but I never received the call back they promised me with an answer, leaving this and a few other technical questions unanswered.</p>

<p><a href="http://www.infograph.com/store/redactitdesktop.asp">Informative Graphics list Redact-It Desktop</a> for only $195. Their website says 1 year of support is included in the purchase price, though I couldn’t find a price for extending that support. <a href="http://www.redact-it.com/download/download1.asp">Short-term trial versions</a> are available for free by email request. For pricing of Redact-It Enterprise Server, contact Informative Graphics’ sales department.</p>

<p>Redact-It’s feature list is tempting, especially at the advertised price.  Their proprietary CSF format and the extra features it offers could be very valuable for another project, but its questionable applicability to this project and their failure to return my call — leaving several important technical questions open — lead me to recommend against this application for current MARBL projects.</p>

<h3>RapidRedact</h3>
<p><a href="http://www.rapidredact.com/">RapidRedact</a> is a redaction-focused tool growing out of Onstream Systems’ Trapeze Desktop image management application. Like Redact-It, it advertises a strong feature list with interesting options for growth.</p>

<p>Like Redact-It, RapidRedact is a standalone program. Though it doesn’t require Acrobat, it does offer plugins to ease intergation into Acrobat and MS Office. RapidRedact also offers a <a href="http://www.rapidredact.com/sdk.asp">Developer’s Toolkit</a> to enable software developers to extend it easily, as well as a <a href="http://www.rapidredact.com/hvsfeatures.asp">High Volume Server</a> to ease batch processing.</p>

<h4>Analysis</h4>
<p>RapidRedact offers a strong <a href="http://www.rapidredact.com/desktopfeatures.asp">feature list</a> to help automate the redaction process. According to its marketing material, it integrates into Microsoft Word to simplify workflow for redacting Word documents. It comes with tools for automatic redaction of several common types of sensitive information in addition to supporting manual review and redaction by MARBL staff. The optional Developer’s Toolkit allows developers to write software for further customization and automation of the redaction process. Their High Volume Server offering could be valuable for automatically redacting sensitive terms from a large number of documents at once.</p>

<p>RapidRedact appears to have only a few drawbacks. From my brief communication with their sales team whether the output format automatically provides searchable text or if additional workflow is necessary to provide it. The Developer’s Toolkit and High Volume Server options sound interesting if the project increases in scope to justify the cost, though it’s not entirely clear how much of the extended functionality would be immediately useful for current MARBL redaction tasks.</p>

<p>The <a href="http://www.rapidredact.com/purchase.asp">pricing and sales structure</a> is a problematic part of RapidRedact’s offering: Unlike other redaction products, RapidRedact offers no pricing online for even their basic product. They sell primarily through resellers and offer only long-distance phone numbers and email for sales contact, though they did respond quickly when I emailed them. Communication and pricing difficulties may be somewhat ameliorated by RapidRedact’s <a href="http://www.kirtas.com/index.php?option=com_content&amp;view=article&amp;id=19&amp;Itemid=38">relationship with Kirtas</a> if the Library still maintains a relationship with that company.</p>

<p>RapidRedact’s feature list and integration opportunities make it a strong offering for current MARBL redaction projects. Its programming and batch operation options provide strong opportunities for future development if project scope grows to demand them. The lack of clarity regarding searchable text workflow is a blemish on this product offering, but ultimately it is only a minor problem for current and anticipated MARBL projects. Thus, insofar as pricing and sales arrangements can be made, RapidRedact is our primary technical recommendation.</p>

<h3>Image Solutions ISIToolBox Professional</h3>
<p><a href="http://www.imagesolutions.com/isitoolbox/">ISIToolBox</a> is a suite of applications for professional production, publication, and review of large batches of PDF documents. One of the applications in the suite targets redaction tasks. It would be worth a closer look if MARBL decided to acquire a large collection of PDF documents. It may also warrant further consideration for Library projects focused on bulk digitization of print documents or on large-scale PDF publication of Library materials. For simply redacting born-digital archives, though — especially those not originally in PDF — this suite is overkill, as reflected in its high price tag.</p>

<h3>Word Redaction Add-in</h3>
<p>Microsoft offer a simple <a href="http://www.microsoft.com/downloads/details.aspx?FamilyId=028C0FD7-67C2-4B51-8E87-65CC9F30F2ED">redaction add-in for MS Word 2000 and 2003</a>. Using this add-in, users may manually highlight a section of a Word document and securely replace it with a colored box. This product is included in our list because it has the advantage of keeping the document in a word processing format, which could simplify the ultimate task of returning redacted documents to their original format and environment. Unfortunately, the add-in has a number of serious drawbacks. Notably, redaction work is entirely manual, and the add-in is not supported in newer versions of Word. Either problem alone would make this product unsuitable for a serious MARBL digital archival project.</p>

<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>This investigation has reviewed a number of redaction programs. Several of them have interesting feature sets that may ultimately make them a valuable part of a larger project preparing documents for redaction and publication in PDF format. For current projects we recommend <a href="http://www.rapidredact.com/">RapidRedact</a> insofar as it proves financially feasible and redaction staff find its interface usable. Failing that, <a href="http://www.appligent.com/redax">Appligent Redax</a> is an excellent second choice and also highly recommended.</p>

<p>Regardless of the product ultimately purchased, we strongly recommend purchase of a standard technical support contract alongside the application.</p>
