<p>MIT and several other institutes released a number of <a href="http://tech.mit.edu/V109/N22/prod.22n.html" target="_blank">studies</a> challenging traditional thinking on Productivity and Rewards in jobs that require cognitive skills or complex thinking showing the value of employee empowerment and the central role it plays in building engaged colleagues.&nbsp; I've run across comments on these studies in a number of books over the last year including The Tipping Point, Good to Great and Stumbling on Happiness and it's shaped my own approach to management dramatically.</p><p>This video does a great job of talking about the overall findings of these studies and application to the modern workplace and in particular I think it's something I think can apply to work here in the library and at the University in general as well as any organization for whom a "brain trust" is vital.</p><p><object width="640" height="385"><param name="movie" value="http://www.youtube.com/v/u6XAPnuFjJc&amp;hl=en_US&amp;fs=1&amp;color1=0x2b405b&amp;color2=0x6b8ab6" /><param name="allowFullScreen" value="true" /><param name="allowscriptaccess" value="always" /><embed type="application/x-shockwave-flash" width="640" height="385" src="http://www.youtube.com/v/u6XAPnuFjJc&amp;hl=en_US&amp;fs=1&amp;color1=0x2b405b&amp;color2=0x6b8ab6" allowscriptaccess="always" allowfullscreen="true"></embed></object></p><p>Obviously the topic is complex as not as simple as "Power to the People" and like many discussions of work practices or behavior it factors out other problems that may be more critical or blocking this as the most critical element to address in developing a productive organization.  Additionally there are a number of misconceptions that this applies to management alone and the empowerment of lower levels of the organization, when this actually needs to be viewed as how we effect each other in all directions across the organization</p>
